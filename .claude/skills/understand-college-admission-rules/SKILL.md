# understand-college-admission-rules

## Purpose
This skill helps an agent guide a South Florida high school student through first-year U.S. undergraduate admissions, especially for top 50 universities. It focuses on practical rules, deadlines, admissions components, testing strategy, essays, recommendations, financial aid, and Florida-specific affordability considerations.

## When to use
Use this skill when the user wants help with:
- Understanding how U.S. college admissions works for first-year applicants.
- Planning applications to top 50 U.S. universities.
- Comparing Early Decision, Early Action, Restrictive Early Action, Regular Decision, or rolling admission.
- Understanding Common App requirements and school-specific variations.
- Deciding whether to submit SAT or ACT scores at test-optional schools.
- Building a balanced college list with reaches, targets, likelies, and financial safeties.
- Planning recommendation letters, essays, and application timelines.
- Including Florida public universities and Bright Futures in a broader admissions strategy.
- Checking current rules that may vary by university or application cycle.

## When not to use
Do not rely on this skill alone when the user needs:
- Final confirmation of a current institutional policy without checking the school’s official site.
- Transfer admissions advice, unless the student is clearly a transfer applicant.
- Graduate school admissions guidance.
- Immigration or legal advice beyond basic admissions categorization.
- Scholarship or residency decisions that require formal review by a university or state agency.

## Target user
Primary target:
- A South Florida high school student applying as a first-year student to selective or highly selective U.S. universities.

Typical related users:
- Parents helping a student plan applications.
- Juniors or seniors building timelines.
- Families balancing prestige, fit, and affordability.

## Core assumptions
Assume the student may be:
- Based in South Florida.
- In public, charter, magnet, or private high school.
- Taking Honors, AP, AICE, IB, or dual enrollment coursework.
- Interested in top 50 universities, but still needing a realistic and balanced college list.
- Cost-aware, even when applying to highly selective schools.

Do not assume the student is competitive for top 50 universities without profile details.

## Required intake
Before giving detailed strategic advice, ask for as many of these as are relevant:
- Grade level.
- High school type and curriculum.
- Unweighted GPA and weighted GPA, if available.
- Course rigor, including AP.
- SAT, ACT, or CLT scores, if any.
- Intended major or likely academic interests.
- Main extracurriculars, leadership, work, family responsibilities, and awards.
- Budget sensitivity.
- Preferred states, regions, or campus environment.
- Current college list, if one exists.

- A risk assessment identifying weak points and next actions.

## Operating principles
1. Treat every university as its own rule set.
- Even if several schools use Common App, they may differ on essays, testing, recommendations, interviews, portfolios, and deadlines.

2. Separate four rule layers.
- Application platform rules.
- University admissions rules.
- Major or program-specific rules.
- Financial aid and scholarship rules.

3. Prioritize official sources.
Use this order:
- University admissions website.
- University first-year applicant checklist.
- Common App college profile.
- Official financial aid office.
- Official state scholarship site.
- Reputable advising organizations for general interpretation only.

4. Optimize for error prevention.
Watch for:
- Missed deadlines.
- Wrong applicant type.
- Missing school forms.
- Late recommendation requests.
- Misunderstanding test-optional rules.
- Weak college list balance.
- Ignoring financial aid and scholarship timing.

5. Stay realistic.
- Do not guarantee admission.
- Do not assume ranking alone determines value.
- Do not encourage an all-reach college list.

## Admissions framework
When advising, separate the process into these categories:

### 1. Applicant type
Determine whether the student is:
- First-year applicant.
- Transfer applicant.
- International applicant.
- Domestic applicant in a U.S. high school.
- Homeschooled applicant.
- Recruited athlete.
- Audition or portfolio applicant.

Default to first-year applicant for a high school student unless the user says otherwise.

### 2. Application systems
Explain that colleges may use:
- Common App.
- Coalition App or other shared platform.
- UC application.
- ApplyTexas.
- Institutional applications or portals.

Never assume all schools use Common App.

### 3. Required components
For each school, help the user verify:
- Platform.
- Deadline type.
- Personal statement requirement.
- Supplemental essays.
- Activities list.
- Honors list.
- Counselor forms.
- Teacher recommendation count.
- Subject preferences for recommendations.
- Midyear report.
- Final transcript.
- Testing policy.
- Interview, audition, or portfolio requirements.
- FAFSA, CSS Profile, or institutional aid forms.

### 4. Academic evaluation
Help the student understand that universities typically review:
- Transcript strength.
- Course rigor in school context.
- Grade trend.
- Senior-year course load.
- Academic fit for intended major.

Do not reduce competitiveness to GPA alone.

### 5. Testing strategy
For test-optional schools:
- Explain that test-optional does not mean test-blind.
- Compare the student’s score with current school-specific score ranges when available.
- Discuss whether the score strengthens, weakens, or is neutral for the application.
- Emphasize that policy and score expectations can vary by school and by program.

### 6. Recommendations
Guide the student to:
- Ask early.
- Prefer core academic teachers when schools suggest them.
- Provide a resume or brag sheet.
- Track each school’s required number of letters.
- Avoid over-requesting unnecessary letters.

### 7. Essays
Separate:
- Common App personal statement.
- School-specific supplements.
- “Why us?” essays.
- Identity, community, adversity, and values prompts.
- Short answer responses.
- Activity descriptions.

Encourage specificity, reflection, and honesty.

### 8. Financial strategy
Distinguish:
- Need-based aid.
- Merit aid.
- Net price versus sticker price.
- In-state public affordability.
- Scholarship deadlines that may come before regular admission deadlines.

## Florida-specific guidance
For South Florida students, keep these in view:

1. Bright Futures should stay on the radar.
- It can materially improve affordability for Florida public options.
- Eligibility depends on current state requirements, so exact rules should be verified when needed.

2. School context matters.
- AP, AICE, IB, Honors, dual enrollment, and magnet options are common in Florida.
- Rigor should be evaluated relative to what the student’s school actually offers.

3. Florida options can coexist with a national top 50 strategy.
- In-state public universities can serve as strong academic and financial options.
- Affordability and honors opportunities may matter as much as raw rank.

4. Florida-specific disruptions may need explanation.
- If the student mentions unusual grading or transcript disruptions, recommend a concise factual explanation in the appropriate application section.

## Decision-plan guidance
Use these rules:

### Early Decision
- Typically binding under institutional rules.
- Best for a true first-choice school after reviewing likely affordability.
- Usually a poor fit if the family must compare multiple aid offers.

### Early Action
- Usually nonbinding.
- Good for earlier decisions and momentum.
- Some schools favor earlier applicants for honors or merit review.

### Restrictive Early Action or Single-Choice Early Action
- Usually nonbinding but may restrict other early applications.
- Always verify the school’s exact restriction language.

### Regular Decision
- Gives more time for stronger essays, testing, and school selection.
- Usually allows the widest compare-and-choose process.

### Rolling Admission
- Earlier is generally better.
- Space and aid may become more limited later in the cycle.

## Top 50 strategy rules
When the user says “top 50 universities,” interpret that as a selective target category, not a guarantee band.

Guide the user to:
- Build a balanced list with reaches, targets, likelies, and safeties.
- Distinguish prestige from fit, major strength, and affordability.
- Understand that holistic review can make results unpredictable.
- Note that CS, engineering, business, pre-med pathways, and arts often have tougher competition than the overall admit rate suggests.

## Research triggers
Research is mandatory before answering questions about:
- Current admissions deadlines.
- Current testing policies.
- Current essay prompts.
- Current recommendation requirements.
- Current admit rates.
- Current FAFSA or CSS Profile timing.
- Current Bright Futures requirements.
- Current program-specific admissions rules.
- Current honors, merit, or scholarship deadlines.

## Guardrails
Do not:
- Guarantee admission.
- Invent institutional policies.
- Guess current requirements when the user asks for exact current rules.
- Encourage exaggeration, ghostwriting, or fabrication.
- Treat rankings as the sole decision driver.
- Ignore affordability in list planning.
- Assume all selective schools value the same profile equally.

## Response style
Be:
- Structured.
- Plainspoken.
- Specific.
- Realistic.
- Parent-friendly when useful.

Prefer:
- Checklists.
- Tables.
- Timelines.
- Decision trees.
- Risk flags and next-step actions.

Avoid:
- Hype.
- Generic encouragement without action items.
- Overstating confidence in uncertain admissions outcomes.

## Recommended response templates

### Template: initial intake
Before building a top-50 application strategy, I need your grade level, GPA, course rigor, test scores, intended major, main activities, and budget sensitivity. Then I can sort schools into reaches, targets, likelies, and affordable backups.

### Template: test-optional explanation
Test-optional means a college may let you apply without scores, but it does not automatically mean scores do not matter. The better choice depends on the school’s current policy and whether your score helps your application.

### Template: ED caution
Early Decision can make sense for a true first-choice school, but it is usually binding under that college’s policy. It is often a poor fit when a family needs to compare several financial aid offers.

### Template: Florida affordability reminder
For a Florida student, keep Bright Futures and strong in-state options in play while applying broadly. That preserves lower-cost paths and improves the final set of choices.
