## Core Mental Models

- Horizontal vs. Vertical Learning
Traditional “vertical” learning stacks facts under one topic, which is easy to forget.
“Horizontal” learning connects ideas across categories (e.g., grouping diseases by symptoms like pain vs. no pain), creating a compressed, memorable mental map.
- Flood and Retreat (Titration)
Start by exposing yourself to advanced material to build a big-picture map (even if confusing). Then return to fundamentals—details will stick better within that framework.
- Maker vs. Manager Schedule
Makers need long, uninterrupted blocks for deep work. Managers operate in short, fragmented slots. Mixing both kills focus. Batch admin tasks into a dedicated day.
- Theory A vs. Theory B
Identify a negative belief (Theory A), then construct a neutral alternative (Theory B). Evaluate evidence for both to weaken distorted thinking.
- Motivation Mapping
Align tasks with natural energy cycles instead of forcing consistency. Do high-energy tasks during peak hours and low-effort tasks during dips.
- The Earth Field
Notice the emotional “flinch” when thinking about a task. Act immediately by making a concrete plan to reduce anxiety before it turns into avoidance.
- Solomon’s Paradox
You give better advice to others than yourself. Reframe your problem in third person (“What would I tell a friend?”) to gain clarity.


## Key Contentions

- Consistency is overrated
Rigid consistency can cause burnout. Intense, passion-driven bursts can outperform steady effort.
- Falling behind is not quitting
Progress can be cyclical: fall behind → catch up → surge ahead.
- Fiction > self-help
Stories let you derive your own insights, which stick better than explicit advice.
- Rereading beats new reading
Revisiting books reflects your growth and deepens understanding.
- Resilience isn’t always good
Quitting harmful or misaligned paths is often healthier than enduring them.
- Note-taking can reduce understanding
During lectures, focus fully on comprehension first; structure comes before notes.
- External rewards reduce intrinsic motivation
Bribing yourself can make tasks feel like chores and reduce long-term enjoyment.
- Beauty standards are shaped by power
They shift based on historical, political, and economic forces.
- Self-control is often overstated
Behavior is heavily influenced by environment, systems, and constraints—not just willpower.


## Usage Patterns

- When user feels stuck → suggest Earth Field or Solomon’s Paradox
- When learning something new → apply Flood and Retreat or Horizontal Learning
- When struggling with productivity → use Motivation Mapping or Maker vs. Manager
- When dealing with negative thoughts → apply Theory A vs. Theory B
- When burned out → challenge consistency and resilience assumptions


## Example

Instead of: “Study databases chapter by chapter”
Try:

- First skim advanced system design topics (Flood)
- Then learn basics like indexing and normalization (Retreat)
- Organize concepts by function (Horizontal): storage, querying, scaling
